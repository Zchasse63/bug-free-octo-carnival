## Training Philosophies

### 1. Polarized Training (80/20)

**Pioneer**: Stephen Seiler (Norwegian sports scientist)

**Core Principle**: ~80% of training volume at low intensity (below ventilatory threshold 1 / lactate threshold 1), ~20% at high intensity (above VT2/LT2). Very little time in the "moderate" middle zone (threshold pace / tempo).

**The Science**:
- Seiler analyzed training logs of elite endurance athletes across rowing, cycling, XC skiing, running — found this distribution emerged naturally among the most successful
- The Esteve-Lanao study (2007): runners who trained with more polarized distribution improved 10K time more than those with a threshold-heavy distribution
- The "avoid the middle" rationale: moderate intensity creates high fatigue cost relative to adaptation stimulus. You get more training volume (and thus more total adaptation) by keeping easy days truly easy

**Who Uses It**:
- Many elite Kenyan and Ethiopian runners naturally run easy at very slow paces between hard sessions
- Widely adopted in Nordic endurance sports (XC skiing, rowing)
- Popularized for recreational runners by Matt Fitzgerald ("80/20 Running")

**Critics Say**:
- The 80/20 split was observed in elites training 15-25+ hours/week — may not apply to someone running 4-5 hours/week
- For time-limited runners, more threshold work may produce better results per hour invested
- "Easy" for an elite may still be 6:30/mi — "easy" for a recreational runner might be walking, which has different physiological demands
- The intensity distribution depends on how you measure it (by time, by session count, by distance)

**Best Suited For**: Runners training 5+ days/week with significant volume. Marathon and ultra runners. Anyone who tends to go too hard on easy days.

### 2. Jack Daniels' VDOT System

**Pioneer**: Jack Daniels, PhD — often called "the world's best running coach"

**Core Principle**: All training paces are derived from your current fitness level (VDOT), which is calculated from a recent race performance. Five training zones, each targeting a specific physiological adaptation:

| Zone | Name | Purpose | % of VDOT Pace |
|------|------|---------|---------------|
| E | Easy | Aerobic base, recovery, running economy | 59-74% VO2max |
| M | Marathon | Specific marathon fitness | ~75-84% VO2max |
| T | Threshold | Improve lactate clearance | ~83-88% VO2max |
| I | Interval | VO2max improvement | ~95-100% VO2max |
| R | Repetition | Speed, running economy, form | Faster than VO2max pace |

**The Science**:
- Based on decades of lab research correlating VO2max, lactate threshold, and running economy
- Each workout type has a specific physiological target — running faster doesn't make it "better"
- Daniels' "minimum effective dose" principle: the slowest pace that produces the desired adaptation is the best training pace (less injury risk, less fatigue)

**Who Uses It**: Extremely widely used by high school, college, and recreational runners worldwide. The VDOT calculator is one of the most popular running tools online.

**Critics Say**:
- VDOT is based on race performance, which can be affected by tactics, weather, course — not purely fitness
- The system is somewhat rigid — experienced runners may need to deviate from calculated paces
- Doesn't account for individual variation in lactate dynamics, HR response, etc.
- Less adaptable to day-to-day readiness than RPE-based systems

**Best Suited For**: Runners who want structure and data-driven pacing. Runners with recent race results. Those training for specific race distances.

### 3. Maffetone Method (MAF)

**Pioneer**: Dr. Phil Maffetone

**Core Principle**: Train exclusively below your Maximum Aerobic Function heart rate (180 minus age, with adjustments) for an extended base-building period. Build a massive aerobic engine before adding any intensity.

**The Formula**: MAF HR = 180 - age
- Subtract 5 if recovering from illness/injury, or if HR medications
- Subtract 5 if just starting or inconsistently training
- Add 5 if training consistently for 2+ years with no injuries/illness

**The Science**:
- Low intensity training preferentially develops fat oxidation, mitochondrial density, capillary networks
- Reduces cortisol and stress hormones compared to chronic high-intensity training
- The MAF test (timed distance at MAF HR) should improve over months — "aerobic development"
- Some evidence that chronic moderate-to-high intensity training can suppress aerobic development

**Who Uses It**:
- Mark Allen (6x Ironman champion) credited Maffetone with his career turnaround
- Popular in the ultrarunning and triathlon communities
- Many recreational runners who were chronically injured from running too hard

**Critics Say**:
- For younger runners, MAF pace can be extremely slow (walking pace for some)
- Months of pure aerobic training with zero intensity can lead to significant neuromuscular detraining
- Running very slowly changes biomechanics — shorter stride, different foot strike, altered arm swing
- Lacks the specificity principle — you need to practice racing speeds to race well
- Heart rate is a poor proxy for actual metabolic state (affected by heat, caffeine, stress, cardiac drift)
- The 180-age formula is a rough heuristic with no strong physiological basis for the specific number

**Best Suited For**: Injury-prone runners who need to slow down. Ultrarunners building enormous aerobic bases. Beginners who need to build consistency without burning out.

### 4. Lydiard Method

**Pioneer**: Arthur Lydiard (New Zealand, 1960s-2000s) — coached Olympic champions Peter Snell, Murray Halberg

**Core Principle**: Structured periodization through distinct training phases:
1. **Base Building** (aerobic conditioning) — 12+ weeks of high-volume running at "comfortable" effort, NOT slow jogging
2. **Hill Resistance** — bounding, springing uphill for leg strength
3. **Anaerobic Development** (sharpening) — track intervals, time trials
4. **Coordination / Taper** — sharpening speed while reducing volume before racing

**Critical Nuance**: Lydiard's "aerobic running" was NOT jogging. He advocated running at the highest pace that remained fully aerobic — what he called "running to feel pleasantly tired." For a fit runner, this could be 7:00/mi, not 10:00/mi. The modern misinterpretation of Lydiard as "run slow" is inaccurate.

**The Science**:
- Periodization allows systematic development of different energy systems
- The base phase builds capillary density, mitochondria, fat oxidation, structural resilience
- The later phases develop speed on top of the aerobic foundation — "you can't fire a cannon from a canoe"
- Lydiard's athletes peaked at championship time, not during training — the periodization was targeted

**Who Uses It**: Many modern coaching systems are descendants of Lydiard. Pfitzinger, Hudson, and others explicitly build on his foundation.

**Critics Say**:
- Strict periodization means you're only "sharp" for a few weeks per year
- The transition between phases can be jarring
- The original system was designed for milers — marathon runners may need different periodization
- Modern thinking favors more concurrent development (some speed year-round)

**Best Suited For**: Runners targeting a specific peak race. Those willing to commit to a multi-month structured cycle. 800m-10K athletes especially.

### 5. Hansons Method

**Pioneer**: Keith and Kevin Hanson (Michigan, coached Hansons-Brooks Distance Project)

**Core Principle**: "Cumulative fatigue" — simulating race-day tiredness through frequent, moderately hard running. The body learns to perform while fatigued. Key features:
- Running 6 days per week (high frequency)
- Long runs capped at 16 miles (not 20-22 like traditional marathon plans)
- "Something of quality" (SOS) days 3x per week: tempo, speed, and long run
- Easy days truly easy, but run on accumulated fatigue from the week

**The 16-Mile Long Run Logic**: At mile 16 of Hansons' plan, your legs already have 40-50 miles of accumulated fatigue from the week. So running 16 miles on tired legs simulates miles 10-26 of a marathon better than running 20 on fresh legs.

**The Science**:
- Glycogen depletion training — running on partially depleted stores teaches the body to adapt
- Higher frequency running maintains consistent training stimulus
- The accumulated fatigue model has parallels in block periodization

**Who Uses It**: Popular among first-time and intermediate marathoners. The Hansons-Brooks Distance Project has produced Olympic Trials qualifiers.

**Critics Say**:
- Six days a week is too much for injury-prone runners
- Never running beyond 16 miles leaves some runners psychologically unprepared for the marathon wall
- The "cumulative fatigue" can become "cumulative breakdown" without adequate recovery
- Some athletes need the confidence of completing a 20-mile run before race day

**Best Suited For**: Consistent runners who can handle 6 days/week. Those who struggle with recovery from very long runs. Marathoners who want a higher-frequency approach.

---

### 6. Pfitzinger (Advanced Marathoning)

**Pioneer**: Pete Pfitzinger — exercise physiologist, 2x US Olympic marathoner

**Core Principle**: Lab-based, physiological approach to marathon training. Plans organized by weekly mileage (e.g., 18/55 = 18 weeks, 55 miles peak). Key innovations:
- **Medium-long runs** (mid-week runs of 11-15 miles) — builds endurance without the recovery cost of a true long run
- **Lactate threshold runs** as the primary quality workout
- Long runs include some at marathon pace
- Detailed recovery protocols

**Key Workout Types**:
- General aerobic (GA): moderate, not easy — building aerobic power
- Endurance: long runs, some with MP segments
- Lactate threshold: 20-40 min at 15K-HM race pace
- VO2max intervals: 3-5 min repeats
- Speed: shorter reps for economy

**The Science**:
- Pfitzinger's background in exercise physiology shows — every workout has a lab-justified purpose
- The medium-long run concept is unique and well-supported (gets 80% of long run benefit at 60% of recovery cost)
- Periodization within the plan (general → specific → taper)

**Who Uses It**: Intermediate to advanced marathoners. Very popular among the 3:00-3:30 marathon crowd.

**Critics Say**:
- Plans can be aggressive — jumping from 55 to 70 mpw is a big step for many
- Less flexibility than adaptive approaches
- Easy paces in the plans are often too fast for many runners
- Doesn't account for cross-training or life stress

**Best Suited For**: Experienced runners (2+ years) targeting a marathon. Those comfortable with 5-6 days of running and moderate-to-high mileage.

### 7. Canova Method

**Pioneer**: Renato Canova — Italian coach of Kenyan/Ethiopian marathon champions

**Core Principle**: "Extension of quality" — gradually increasing the volume of race-specific work over a training block. Three periods:
1. **Fundamental** — general aerobic and strength work
2. **Special** — introducing race-pace work in increasing amounts
3. **Specific** — high volumes of race-specific work (e.g., marathon pace runs of 30-40km)

**Unique Concepts**:
- **Progressive long runs**: Start easy, finish at marathon pace or faster (the last 30-40% at target pace)
- **Fast continuous runs**: Extended runs at marathon pace (25-35km)
- **Specific blocks**: 2-3 week blocks of very high specific work, followed by recovery
- Recovery runs are genuinely easy — 8:00-9:00/mi even for 2:05 marathoners

**The Science**:
- Specificity principle taken to its extreme — the body adapts to exactly what it practices
- The progressive long run teaches pace management and finishing strength
- High-volume specific work pushes lactate threshold closer to VO2max pace

**Who Uses It**: Elite Kenyan and Ethiopian marathon runners. Several world record holders.

**Critics Say**:
- Extremely demanding — designed for professional athletes with no other life stress
- The high-volume specific work is risky for injury-prone runners
- Requires years of base building before attempting Canova-style workouts
- Not easily adaptable to recreational runners

**Best Suited For**: Advanced runners with a large aerobic base, marathon-focused, and able to handle high training loads.

### 8. Norwegian Double Threshold

**Pioneer**: Gjert Ingebrigtsen (father/coach of Henrik, Filip, Jakob Ingebrigtsen); Marius Bakken (Norwegian exercise physiologist)

**Core Principle**: Two threshold-intensity sessions per day, most days of the week. But "threshold" means at or just below lactate threshold — controlled, not hard. Lactate is measured during sessions to ensure the right intensity.

**Typical Week**:
- 10-13 sessions per week
- 4-5 of those are threshold sessions
- Remaining are easy running
- Threshold sessions: 5-6 × 6min at threshold, or 4 × 8-10min
- Lactate tested during intervals — target is 2-4 mmol/L (comfortably sustainable)
- The intensity is described as "comfortably hard" — not all-out

**The Science**:
- Based on research showing lactate threshold is the strongest predictor of endurance performance (more than VO2max)
- High frequency of threshold stimulus → rapid lactate threshold improvement
- The moderate intensity (not maximal) allows high frequency without burnout
- Lactate-guided training prevents the athlete from going too hard

**Who Uses It**: Jakob Ingebrigtsen (Olympic 1500m champion), the Norwegian national team. Increasingly adopted by middle-distance runners worldwide.

**Critics Say**:
- Requires lactate testing equipment and expertise
- Two-a-day training is impractical for most non-professional runners
- May not develop VO2max as well as true high-intensity intervals
- The method is proven for middle distance (800m-5K) — less evidence for marathon
- Risks overtraining if the athlete pushes intensity above threshold

**Best Suited For**: Middle-distance runners (800m-5K) with the time for two-a-day training. Athletes with access to lactate testing. Those who respond well to threshold work.

### 9. Effort-Based / "Run by Feel" Training

**Core Principle**: Use perceived effort (RPE) rather than pace or heart rate to guide training. The body integrates all stressors (sleep, heat, altitude, fatigue, stress) into one feeling — trust that feeling.

**The Borg Scale** (Rate of Perceived Exertion):
- 1-2: Very light (walking)
- 3-4: Light to moderate (easy running, conversational)
- 5-6: Moderate to somewhat hard (tempo effort)
- 7-8: Hard (interval effort)
- 9: Very hard (race effort)
- 10: Maximal (sprint, unsustainable)

**The Science**:
- RPE correlates well with metabolic intensity across conditions
- Unlike HR, RPE isn't affected by cardiac lag, drift, caffeine, or medication
- Unlike pace, RPE automatically adjusts for hills, wind, heat, fatigue
- Tim Noakes' "Central Governor" theory: the brain regulates effort to protect the body — RPE is the brain's output signal

**Who Uses It**: Many elite runners train primarily by feel, using pace and HR as secondary references. Brad Hudson advocates for adaptive, feel-based training. Many East African runners don't use watches at all during training.

**Critics Say**:
- Beginners don't have calibrated RPE — they don't know what "easy" feels like
- Some personality types always push too hard regardless of perceived effort
- Lacks the objectivity and trackability of pace/HR
- Hard to program specific workouts without objective metrics

**Best Suited For**: Experienced runners with good self-awareness. Those who tend to be overly data-dependent. Ultra-distance runners where pace is less meaningful.

### 10. Heart Rate Zone Training

**Core Principle**: Train by heart rate zones to ensure the right physiological stimulus regardless of pace. Different zone systems exist:

| System | Zone 1 | Zone 2 | Zone 3 | Zone 4 | Zone 5 |
|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| Seiler (3-zone) | Below VT1 | Between VT1-VT2 | Above VT2 | — | — |
| Daniels (5-zone) | Easy | Marathon | Threshold | Interval | Repetition |
| Karvonen | 50-60% HRR | 60-70% HRR | 70-80% HRR | 80-90% HRR | 90-100% HRR |
| Maffetone | Below MAF | — | — | — | — |

**The Science**:
- Heart rate directly reflects cardiovascular load
- Zone 2 (aerobic) training develops mitochondrial density, fat oxidation, capillary networks
- Zone 4-5 training develops VO2max, lactate clearance, anaerobic capacity
- HR zones can be personalized via lab testing (lactate threshold test, VO2max test)

**Critics Say** (the zone 2 debate — see below):
- HR lags intensity changes by 30-90 seconds — useless for short intervals
- HR is affected by heat, caffeine, dehydration, stress, sleep, altitude, cardiac drift
- "Zone 2" training at very slow paces may alter running biomechanics negatively
- HR zones calculated from 220-age are inaccurate for many individuals
- Over-reliance on HR can cause runners to ignore useful body signals (RPE)

**Best Suited For**: Runners who tend to go too hard on easy days (HR forces them to slow down). Those with HR monitors. Runners in hot/humid climates where pace is misleading.

### 11. Power-Based Running (Stryd)

**Pioneer**: Stryd (foot pod), also Apple Watch Ultra (wrist estimate), Garmin Running Power

**Core Principle**: Running power (watts) provides a real-time measure of mechanical output that isn't affected by hills, wind, heat, or cardiac drift. Train by power zones similar to cycling power training.

**Advantages Over Pace**: On a hill, pace drops but power stays constant if effort is constant. In wind, same effect. Power shows actual work output.

**Advantages Over HR**: No lag. Not affected by temperature, caffeine, dehydration. Responds instantly to changes in effort.

**The Science**:
- Running power captures external mechanical work (ground contact forces)
- Power duration curves can estimate critical power (CP) — the running equivalent of cycling's FTP
- Still a newer metric — less decades of research compared to HR and pace

**Critics Say**:
- Running power is NOT the same as cycling power — there's no universally agreed measurement standard
- Different devices measure different things (Stryd measures ground force, Apple Watch estimates from accelerometer)
- Running economy varies between runners — two runners at the same watts may be running very different paces
- Adds complexity without proven superiority over RPE + pace for most runners
- The science is still developing — power zones for running are less validated than for cycling

**Best Suited For**: Data-driven runners who train across varied terrain. Hilly course racing. Those already familiar with power training from cycling.

## The Key Debates

### Debate 1: Does Zone 2 Training Make You Slow?

**The Argument For Zone 2**:
- Builds the aerobic engine (mitochondria, capillaries, fat oxidation)
- Low injury risk, low cortisol
- Allows higher total training volume
- The vast majority of elite runners do most miles at easy effort
- Stephen Seiler's research consistently shows polarized > threshold-heavy

**The Argument Against (or "Zone 2 Hype")**:
- Chris Hinshaw and others argue that for time-limited athletes, more intensity per session is more efficient
- Running very slowly (especially for smaller/lighter runners whose MAF pace is near walking) may change running mechanics — shorter stride, higher ground contact time, more braking forces
- The "teach your body to run slow" concern: neural pathways are trained at the paces you practice
- Elite runners' "easy" pace (6:30-7:30/mi) is still mechanically running; recreational "zone 2" (10:00-12:00/mi) may be biomechanically different
- The original 80/20 research was on elites doing 15-25 hrs/week — someone doing 4-5 hrs/week may need different distribution

**The Nuanced View**:
- Zone 2 is not inherently slow — it's a metabolic zone, and the pace depends on fitness
- As aerobic fitness improves, zone 2 pace gets faster
- The real problem is "grey zone" training — running moderate every day (too hard for recovery, too easy for adaptation)
- Most recreational runners don't run easy enough on easy days OR hard enough on hard days
- For time-limited runners, a pyramidal distribution (more threshold, less strict 80/20) may be optimal

### Debate 2: Polarized vs Pyramidal vs Threshold-Focused

**Polarized**: 80% low / 0-5% moderate / 15-20% high — "avoid the middle"
**Pyramidal**: 70-80% low / 15-20% moderate / 5-10% high — more threshold work than polarized
**Threshold-focused**: 50-60% low / 25-35% moderate / 10-15% high — heavy tempo/threshold emphasis

**Current Evidence**:
- For high-volume athletes: polarized appears superior (Stöggl & Sperlich 2014)
- For time-limited athletes: pyramidal may be more effective (less total volume but higher moderate intensity)
- For specific race prep: threshold-focused may peak performance short-term
- Individual variation is massive — some respond better to different distributions
- The best coaches (Daniels, Canova, Hudson) use elements of all three at different training phases

### Debate 3: High Mileage vs Quality Over Quantity

**High Mileage Camp**: Running more miles, even slowly, builds structural resilience, aerobic capacity, and running economy through sheer repetition. Lydiard, Canova, and most Kenyan coaches favor high volume.

**Quality Camp**: For time-limited runners, fewer but harder miles can produce similar fitness gains. Hinshaw, some CrossFit endurance coaches, and high-intensity interval training (HIIT) advocates lean this way.

**The Science**: Both work. The optimal balance depends on:
- Available training time
- Injury history (high mileage requires structural readiness)
- Training age (years of consistent running)
- Life stress (high-volume training adds physiological stress)
- Goal race distance (marathon favors volume, 5K can benefit from quality focus)

### Debate 4: Periodization vs Consistent Training

**Periodized**: Distinct phases (base → build → peak → taper), training emphasis changes throughout the year. Lydiard, Canova, traditional coaching.

**Consistent**: Maintain a similar training structure year-round, varying volume slightly. Many recreational runners, Maffetone approach.

**Block Periodization**: Short 2-3 week blocks of focused training (all threshold, or all VO2max work) followed by recovery. Issurin, some modern approaches.

**Current Thinking**: Most modern coaches use some form of periodization but with more overlap between phases than Lydiard's strict model. The trend is toward "concurrent" training (maintaining all systems year-round while emphasizing one).

## The Science Layer

### VO2max

**What It Is**: Maximum rate of oxygen consumption during maximal exercise. Measured in mL/kg/min. The "engine size" of your aerobic system.

**Typical Values**:
- Untrained male: 35-45 mL/kg/min
- Recreational runner: 45-55
- Competitive age-group: 55-65
- Elite: 65-80+
- Kipchoge: ~78 mL/kg/min

**How to Improve It**:
- VO2max intervals: 3-5 min repeats at 95-100% VO2max pace (roughly 3K-5K race pace)
- 2-3 sessions per week targeting VO2max produces significant improvement in 6-8 weeks
- Returns diminish after ~8-12 weeks of VO2max-focused training
- Genetics set the ceiling; training determines how close you get

**Estimating from Running Data**: Daniels' VDOT tables estimate VO2max from race performance. A 20:00 5K ≈ VDOT 52 ≈ VO2max ~55 mL/kg/min (approximate, not exact).

**Key Insight**: VO2max matters but it's not everything. Running economy and lactate threshold determine what percentage of VO2max you can sustain. Kipchoge doesn't have the highest VO2max — he has extraordinary economy and threshold.

---

### Lactate Threshold

**What It Is**: The exercise intensity at which lactate accumulation in the blood begins to increase exponentially above baseline. Two key thresholds:
- **LT1 (aerobic threshold)**: ~2 mmol/L lactate, the upper limit of "easy" running
- **LT2 (anaerobic threshold)**: ~4 mmol/L lactate, roughly the pace you can sustain for 1 hour (close to half-marathon pace for trained runners)

**Why It Matters**: Your lactate threshold determines what fraction of your VO2max you can sustain. An untrained person might reach LT2 at 60% of VO2max; an elite might reach it at 85-90%.

**How to Train It**:
- Tempo runs: 20-40 min at LT2 pace (comfortably hard, conversational in short bursts)
- Cruise intervals: 5-8 min repeats at LT2 with short rest (1 min)
- Progressive runs: starting easy, finishing at threshold
- The Norwegian method: frequent threshold sessions at moderate intensity

---

### Running Economy

**What It Is**: How much oxygen you consume at a given pace. Better economy = less oxygen needed = more efficient. Like fuel economy in a car.

**What Affects It**:
- Biomechanics (stride length, ground contact time, vertical oscillation)
- Muscle fiber composition and tendon stiffness
- Bodyweight (lighter = more economical per kg)
- Training volume (more miles = better economy through repetition)
- Strength training (stiffer tendons store and return more elastic energy)
- Altitude training (increases hemoglobin, delivering more O2 per heartbeat)
- Shoes (super shoes with carbon plates improve economy 2-4%)

**Key Insight**: Economy is trainable and is where shoes, form, strength work, and consistent mileage make their biggest impact. Two runners with identical VO2max can have very different race performances based on economy.

---

### Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

**What It Is**: The variation in time between consecutive heartbeats. Higher HRV generally indicates better recovery and parasympathetic nervous system dominance.

**How to Use It for Training Decisions**:
- Measure first thing in the morning, lying down, before getting up
- Track 7-day rolling average (single-day values are noisy)
- A significant drop (>15-20% below baseline) may indicate: overtraining, illness, poor sleep, high stress
- Use as a "readiness" indicator, not an absolute prescription
- WHOOP, Oura Ring, and Garmin all track HRV

**What the Science Says**:
- HRV-guided training (modifying intensity based on daily HRV) shows modest performance benefits in some studies
- More useful as a "red flag" detector than a daily decision-maker
- Individual baseline matters more than absolute numbers
- Trend direction (improving vs declining over weeks) is more meaningful than daily fluctuations

**For Our AI Coach**: HRV data (from WHOOP integration) should be one input among many for readiness assessment, not the sole determinant. "Your HRV has been trending down for a week — combined with your elevated easy-run HR and yesterday's feedback about heavy legs, let's dial it back today."

---

### Training Stress Balance (CTL/ATL/TSB)

**What It Is**: The Banister impulse-response model for managing training load:
- **CTL (Chronic Training Load)** = "Fitness" — 42-day exponential weighted average of daily training load
- **ATL (Acute Training Load)** = "Fatigue" — 7-day exponential weighted average
- **TSB (Training Stress Balance)** = "Form" — CTL minus ATL. Positive = fresh, Negative = fatigued

**Training Load Sources**:
- **TRIMP** (Training Impulse): HR-based load calculation
- **TSS** (Training Stress Score): Power-based (cycling) or pace-based equivalent
- **Suffer Score**: Strava's proprietary version (available via API)
- **hrTSS**: HR-based TSS estimate

**How Coaches Use It**:
- Ramping CTL = building fitness
- TSB between -10 and -30 = productive training (accumulating fitness)
- TSB between 0 and +25 = fresh, good for racing
- TSB below -30 = high risk of overtraining/injury
- Taper: reduce ATL while CTL stays high → TSB rises → peak form

**Limitations**:
- Doesn't account for training type (a 2-hour easy run and a 2-hour interval session can have similar TSS but very different recovery costs)
- Individual variation in recovery capacity
- Life stress not captured
- The 42-day and 7-day constants are averages — some respond faster/slower

---

### Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE)

The Borg CR-10 scale (modified for running):
- **1-2**: Walking / very easy jogging
- **3**: Easy — could hold a conversation indefinitely
- **4**: Moderate easy — conversation comfortable, slightly more effort
- **5**: Moderate — conversation in sentences, some effort
- **6**: Moderately hard — conversation in phrases
- **7**: Hard — only a few words at a time (threshold/tempo effort)
- **8**: Very hard — race effort for 15-30 min
- **9**: Extremely hard — race effort for 5-10 min
- **10**: Maximum — sprint, 30s to 1 min

**Why It Matters**: RPE integrates all physiological and psychological factors. It's the only metric that automatically accounts for sleep, stress, nutrition, weather, altitude, fatigue, and illness simultaneously.

---

### Aerobic vs Anaerobic Energy Systems

All running uses BOTH systems simultaneously, in different proportions:

| Race Distance | % Aerobic | % Anaerobic |
|--------------|-----------|-------------|
| 800m | ~60% | ~40% |
| 1500m | ~75% | ~25% |
| 5K | ~90% | ~10% |
| 10K | ~95% | ~5% |
| Marathon | ~99% | ~1% |

**Key Insight**: Even the 800m is predominantly aerobic. This is why aerobic development is the foundation for ALL race distances. The debate is about how much anaerobic work to layer on top.

---

### Supercompensation

**The Principle**: After a training stress, the body recovers to baseline, then briefly "supercompensates" above baseline. If the next training stimulus hits during supercompensation, fitness ratchets up. If it hits too early (still recovering) or too late (returned to baseline), the effect is diminished or lost.

**Timeline** (approximate):
- Easy run: recovers in 24-48 hours
- Tempo/threshold: 48-72 hours
- VO2max intervals: 48-96 hours
- Long run: 72-120+ hours
- Race: 1-3+ weeks

**For Training Design**: This is why hard/easy alternation works — the hard session provides stimulus, the easy session allows supercompensation, and the next hard session builds on the elevated baseline.

## Synthesis for Our AI Coach

### What ALL Great Coaches Agree On

1. **Easy days must be easy**. The biggest mistake recreational runners make is running too hard on easy days. Every coach — Daniels, Lydiard, Canova, Maffetone, Hanson — agrees on this.

2. **Consistency beats intensity**. 5 runs/week for 12 months beats 7 runs/week for 3 months then injury. Every training philosophy works only if you can sustain it.

3. **Specificity matters**. To race fast at 5K, you need some training at 5K pace. To marathon well, you need marathon-pace work. The aerobic base supports this but doesn't replace it.

4. **Progressive overload**. Training load must increase gradually over time (the 10% rule is a rough guide, not a law).

5. **Recovery is training**. Adaptation happens during recovery, not during the workout. Sleep is the most powerful recovery tool.

6. **Individuality**. No single system works best for everyone. Age, genetics, injury history, training age, life stress, and recovery capacity all matter.

### What They DISAGREE On

1. **How much easy vs hard**: 80/20? 70/30? It depends on the individual and their training volume.
2. **How slow is "easy"**: Maffetone says very slow. Lydiard says as fast as you can while staying aerobic. The truth depends on the runner's fitness.
3. **Zone 2 pace and biomechanics**: Legitimate concern for slow runners whose zone 2 is walking/shuffling. Less concern for fit runners whose zone 2 is still mechanically running.
4. **Threshold frequency**: Norwegians say almost daily. Daniels says 1-2x/week. Polarized says minimal.
5. **Mileage vs intensity for time-limited runners**: Genuine trade-off with no universal answer.
6. **Periodization model**: Strict phases vs concurrent training vs block periodization.

### How Our AI Coach Should Handle This

The coach should:
1. **Not preach one philosophy**. Be aware of all approaches and their evidence.
2. **Match the approach to the individual**. High-volume runner? More polarized. Time-limited? More pyramidal. Injury-prone? More Maffetone-style base building.
3. **Use data to determine what's working**. If a runner's HR drift is improving on easy runs, the aerobic development is working regardless of which "method" they're following.
4. **Be transparent about trade-offs**. "Running this slow might feel boring and may change your form slightly, but the aerobic benefits are worth it for the next 6 weeks. After that, we'll add speed work."
5. **Adapt the plan based on response**, not the calendar. If the runner is recovering faster than expected, push harder. If they're struggling, back off. This is the core advantage over static plans.
6. **Use RPE as the tiebreaker**. When pace says one thing and HR says another and the runner says a third — trust the runner's feel while noting the discrepancies for pattern analysis.
