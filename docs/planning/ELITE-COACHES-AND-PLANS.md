## 1. Fitness / Exercise Science Experts

### 1.1 Andy Galpin

**Who:** Exercise physiologist, PhD, professor of kinesiology. Co-host of "Perform" podcast. Known for his guest series on the Huberman Lab podcast.

**Core Philosophy on Endurance Training:**

Galpin classifies endurance into distinct physiological categories, each requiring different training protocols:

- **Muscular endurance** -- ability to repeat submaximal contractions (e.g., high-rep bodyweight exercises, circuit training)
- **Anaerobic capacity** -- sustaining maximal-effort work for 30-120 seconds (sprint intervals, HIIT)
- **Maximum aerobic output (VO2max)** -- the ceiling of oxygen utilization (3-8 minute intervals at near-max effort)
- **Long-duration endurance / "Zone 2"** -- sustained effort below lactate threshold, the foundation of health and performance

**Zone Training Framework:**

Galpin emphasizes that zone definitions vary across systems, but his practical framework:

- **Zone 1:** Easy walking. Essentially no training stimulus.
- **Zone 2:** The intensity at which conversation is possible but requires effort. This is the "workhorse" zone. Galpin recommends 150-180 minutes per week minimum for health, more for performance.
- **Zone 3:** "No man's land" -- too hard to be easy, too easy to be hard. Galpin warns most recreational runners spend too much time here.
- **Zone 4-5:** Threshold and above. Reserved for structured quality sessions.

**Key Galpin Protocols for Runners:**

1. **Zone 2 base:** Minimum 150-180 min/week of nasal-breathing-possible cardio
2. **VO2max work:** 4-8 intervals of 3-5 minutes at 90-95% max HR, 1x/week
3. **Recovery:** Sleep 7-9 hours, prioritize protein (1g/lb body weight), hydration
4. **Strength training:** 2x/week minimum, focus on compound movements -- squats, deadlifts, lunges. Not bodybuilding volume -- 3-5 sets of 3-5 reps for strength.

**Notable Quote:**
> "The number one predictor of all-cause mortality reduction is your VO2max. Training it isn't optional -- it's the most important thing you can do for longevity."

**Relevance for AI Coach:** Galpin provides the scientific backing for why easy running matters, why VO2max intervals are non-negotiable, and why strength training belongs in every runner's program. His zone framework is more accessible than lab-based lactate testing.

### 1.2 Andrew Huberman

**Who:** Neuroscientist, Stanford professor, host of Huberman Lab podcast. Not a running coach, but his work on breathing, mental performance, and endurance neuroscience is directly applicable.

**Key Contributions to Running Performance:**

**Breathing Protocols:**
- **Nasal breathing during easy runs:** Huberman advocates nasal breathing as both a pacing tool and a physiological enhancer. Nasal breathing increases nitric oxide production, which improves oxygen delivery. If you can't breathe through your nose, you're going too hard for an easy run.
- **Cyclic sighing for recovery:** Double inhale through the nose, long exhale through the mouth. Proven to lower cortisol faster than meditation. Use post-run or between intervals.
- **"Physiological sigh" during running:** When CO2 builds up and you feel the urge to gasp, a double inhale + extended exhale resets the respiratory pattern without breaking rhythm.
- **Diaphragm and intercostal strengthening:** Huberman emphasizes that respiratory muscles fatigue independently -- training them (e.g., through breath holds, resistance breathing) improves endurance performance.

**Mental Performance & Dopamine:**
- **Visual focus narrows = effort increases.** Looking at a focal point ahead of you triggers a state of heightened alertness and effort. Useful for races and hard intervals.
- **Panoramic vision = effort decreases.** Softening your gaze to peripheral vision activates the parasympathetic system. Useful for easy runs and recovery.
- **Dopamine management:** Huberman warns against "dopamine stacking" (music + caffeine + pre-workout before every run). This erodes the dopamine response over time. Occasional runs without stimuli teach the brain to find reward in the effort itself.

**Endurance-Specific Insights:**
- **"Behind the wall" is neurological, not just metabolic.** The bonk in a marathon has a massive neural fatigue component. Training the brain to tolerate discomfort is as important as training the body to oxidize fat.
- **Non-sleep deep rest (NSDR):** 10-20 minutes of yoga nidra or body-scan meditation post-hard-session accelerates recovery more than passive sitting.

**Relevance for AI Coach:** Huberman's breathing protocols should be embedded in easy run guidance. The dopamine/mental performance insights should inform how the coach talks about effort perception, race-day strategy, and motivation.

### 1.3 Chris Hinshaw

**Who:** CrossFit's Subject Matter Expert in Aerobic Capacity. Former professional Ironman triathlete. Coached 35+ CrossFit Games champions.

**Philosophy: "Aerobic Capacity Is the Limiting Factor"**

Hinshaw's core argument: Most athletes who think they have a "strength" problem actually have an "engine" problem. A bigger aerobic base means faster recovery between efforts, higher sustainable power output, and better performance in everything.

**Interval Design Principles:**

Hinshaw's approach to intervals is distinctive:

1. **Work-to-rest ratios matter more than pace.** He designs intervals around specific energy systems:
   - **Phosphocreatine system (0-10 sec):** Max effort sprints, full recovery (1:10+ rest ratio)
   - **Glycolytic (10-120 sec):** Hard intervals, moderate recovery (1:2-1:3 rest ratio)
   - **Oxidative (2+ min):** Sustained hard effort, shorter recovery (1:1 or less rest ratio)

2. **"Pacing is a skill."** Hinshaw insists that most athletes fail intervals not because they lack fitness but because they go out too fast. He prescribes target paces and penalizes athletes who exceed them.

3. **Mixed-modal conditioning:** Unique to CrossFit context, but the principle translates -- combining running with other movements (rowing, cycling) builds a more resilient aerobic system with less overuse injury risk.

**Key Workouts:**
- **"Death by 10 meters"**: Start at 10m, add 10m every minute. Run each distance, rest the remainder of the minute. Teaches pacing discipline.
- **3x2000m @ 10K effort, 3:00 rest:** Classic aerobic power builder
- **10x400m descending rest (90s, 80s, 70s...):** Forces the aerobic system to recover faster each round

**Relevance for AI Coach:** Hinshaw's energy-system-specific interval design is excellent for structuring quality sessions. His emphasis on pacing discipline should inform how we prescribe and evaluate interval performance.

### 1.4 Max Jolliffe

**Who:** Ultra-endurance athlete, content creator, Moab 240 course record holder. Known for unconventional training approaches and pushing volume boundaries.

**Training Approach:**

Jolliffe's philosophy centers on radical consistency and high volume:

- **"Consistency over a long period will give you greater results than anything else in training. As long as you continually push yourself, you will see progress."**
- Heavily uses stair climber and vertical gain training for ultra preparation
- Emphasizes that ultra training is a "team sport" -- pacers, crew, and support are integral to performance
- Trains with extremely high volume (200+ mile weeks in ultra prep)
- Uses minimal technology -- runs largely by feel rather than pace/HR targets

**Relevance for AI Coach:** Jolliffe represents the ultra-endurance end of the spectrum. His consistency-first philosophy aligns with every major coaching tradition. For our AI coach, the lesson is: the best ability is availability, and the best plan is the one you can actually execute week after week.

## 2. Elite Running Coaches

### 2.1 Jack Daniels

**Who:** Exercise physiologist, Olympic coach, author of "Daniels' Running Formula." Considered by many the greatest running coach in history. Developed the VDOT system.

**The VDOT System:**

VDOT (V-dot-O2max) is a single number that represents the intersection of a runner's VO2max and running economy. It is calculated from race performances and maps to five training zones:

| Zone | Name | Purpose | Example (VDOT 45 / ~22:30 5K) |
|------|------|---------|-------------------------------|
| E | Easy | Aerobic base, recovery, injury prevention | 9:00-9:45/mile |
| M | Marathon | Marathon-specific endurance | ~8:10/mile |
| T | Threshold | Lactate clearance, "comfortably hard" | ~7:25/mile |
| I | Interval | VO2max development | ~6:50/mile |
| R | Repetition | Speed, running economy, neuromuscular | ~6:25/mile or faster |

**Training Distribution According to Daniels:**
- 80-85% of weekly volume at E pace
- 15-20% at quality paces (T, I, R, M)
- Never more than 10% of weekly mileage at I pace in a single session
- R-pace running should be ~5% of weekly mileage
- T-pace work: 20-40 minutes sustained, or cruise intervals (e.g., 4x8min with 1min rest)

**Daniels' Phase System (for a typical 24-week training cycle):**

| Phase | Duration | Focus | Key Workouts |
|-------|----------|-------|-------------|
| Phase I | 6 weeks | Base building | Easy runs, strides, light R work |
| Phase II | 6 weeks | Early quality | R + I intervals, building VO2max |
| Phase III | 6 weeks | Transition quality | T (threshold) emphasis, some I work |
| Phase IV | 6 weeks | Race-specific | M pace for marathon, I pace for 5K/10K |

**Key Daniels Principles:**
1. **"Train at the right pace for the right purpose."** Every run has an intended physiological benefit. Running E runs too fast is the #1 mistake.
2. **Reassess VDOT every 4-6 weeks.** As fitness improves, paces should update. A static plan leaves speed on the table.
3. **The 3-week rule:** Don't increase weekly mileage by more than ~10% per week, and hold at new levels for at least 3 weeks before increasing again.
4. **Quality sessions max 2-3 per week.** More quality = more breakdown risk without proportional gains.

**Notable Quote:**
> "Easy runs are easy. Marathon pace is comfortable. Threshold pace is comfortably hard. Interval pace is hard. Repetition pace is fast."

**Relevance for AI Coach:** The VDOT system is the gold standard for pace prescription. Our AI coach should use race results to calculate VDOT and prescribe E/M/T/I/R paces. The phase system provides a clean periodization framework. Daniels' insistence on purpose-driven pacing is foundational.

### 2.2 Arthur Lydiard

**Who:** New Zealand coach (1917-2004). Trained Olympic gold medalists Peter Snell and Murray Halberg. Considered the father of modern distance training. Revolutionized the concept of aerobic base building.

**The Lydiard System -- Periodization:**

Lydiard's system is built on a sequential progression through distinct phases. The key innovation: even 800m/1500m runners need a massive aerobic base.

| Phase | Duration | Focus | Weekly Structure |
|-------|----------|-------|-----------------|
| **Base Building (Aerobic Conditioning)** | 10-14 weeks (up to 6 months for serious athletes) | Develop maximum aerobic capacity through high-volume running | 100+ miles/week. Mix of long runs (22-28 miles), steady-state runs, and shorter faster aerobic efforts. NOT all slow -- includes running at "best aerobic effort" (close to marathon pace). |
| **Hill Phase (Strength)** | 4-6 weeks | Build leg strength and power through hill circuits | Hill bounding, springing, steep hill repeats. Still maintaining aerobic volume. |
| **Anaerobic (Track Sharpening)** | 4-6 weeks | Develop speed and anaerobic capacity | Track intervals, repetition work at race pace and faster. Volume decreases. |
| **Integration / Coordination** | 2-4 weeks | Bring all elements together | Time trials, tune-up races, race-pace work. |
| **Freshening / Taper** | 10-14 days | Arrive at race day sharp and rested | Reduced volume, maintained intensity. |

**Critical Misconception About Lydiard:**

Many coaches reduce Lydiard to "long slow distance." This is wrong. Lydiard's base phase includes significant effort-based running:

> "The base phase is not all slow running. The longer it progresses, the less slow running there is."

His "conditioning" runs in the base phase were done at "best aerobic effort" -- essentially the fastest pace you can sustain while staying aerobic. For elite runners, this was close to marathon pace. Easy days were truly easy, but 3-4 runs per week had genuine effort.

**Key Lydiard Workouts:**
- **The 22-miler at best aerobic effort:** The signature Lydiard workout. Not a shuffle -- a hard aerobic effort over a hilly course.
- **Hill circuits:** 1-2 mile circuit including bounding up steep hills, springing (short, explosive steps up moderate hills), and running fast on the flat. Full circuit repeated 3-6 times.
- **Windsprints/Strides:** 100m accelerations incorporated into easy runs 2-3x/week even during base phase.

**Lydiard's 6-Month Periodization Example:**

- Months 1-3: Aerobic base. Build from 60 to 100+ miles/week. Long runs progressively faster.
- Month 4: Hill phase. Maintain 80-90 miles/week. 3x/week hill circuits. Still doing a long run.
- Month 5: Track phase. Drop to 60-70 miles. 3x/week track sessions (mile repeats, 800s, 400s).
- Month 5.5-6: Coordination + freshening. 2 tune-up races. Volume drops to 50-60. One quality session per week.

**Relevance for AI Coach:** Lydiard's base-building principles are universal. Our AI coach should insist on an adequate aerobic base before introducing intensity. The sequential periodization model is clean and effective for goal-race training. The correction that base training isn't all slow is critical -- it should include effort-based aerobic running.

### 2.3 Renato Canova

**Who:** Italian coach who has trained more sub-2:05 marathoners than any other coach in history. Worked with Kenyans and Ethiopians including Abel Kirui, Geoffrey Kirui, Florence Kiplagat, Wilson Kipsang, and Sondre Nordstad Moen. Over 50 Olympic and World Championship medalists.

**Core Philosophy: "Extension of Quality, Not Qualification of Volume"**

Canova's most revolutionary insight: Traditional marathon training starts with volume and tries to add quality. Canova starts with quality (race-specific intensity) and extends its duration.

> "What does a 2-hour easy run have to do with the marathon? Nothing."

> "The only mistake in training is not what we do, it is what we don't do."

**Canova's Training Phases:**

| Phase | Duration | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Transition** | 4 weeks (post-marathon) | Recovery, rebuild strength. Easy runs <1 hour, gym work, reactivity exercises. |
| **General** | 4 weeks | General resistance, long runs at 80% MP, fartlek, hills. 13 sessions/week for elites. |
| **Fundamental** | 6 weeks | The base for everything. Long runs at 87-93% MP. Circuit training for strength-endurance. Introduction of "special speeds" (90% and 110% MP). |
| **Specific** | 6-8 weeks | Marathon-specific work. Race pace +/- 10%. This is where the magic happens. |

**Special Speeds (90% and 110% of Marathon Pace):**

- **90% MP** (~20-25 sec/mile slower than goal pace): Maximizes "aerobic fat power" -- the absolute rate of fat oxidation. The strongest physiological justification for long, fast runs.
- **110% MP** (~15-20 sec/mile faster, roughly 10K effort): Targets fast-twitch Type IIa fibers. Slightly exceeds aerobic capabilities, forcing new mitochondrial adaptations.

**The "Special Block" -- Canova's Signature:**

Every 3-4 weeks, Canova prescribes a special block: two quality sessions in one day, totaling 45-50km for elites. Minimal carbohydrate intake between sessions forces fat adaptation.

Example Special Block (elite):
- **Morning (26km):** 3km warm-up, 10km at half-marathon to marathon pace, 10km slightly faster, 3km cool-down
- **Afternoon (24km):** 3km warm-up, 10km at HM-to-MP pace, 8x1km at 5-10K pace, 3km cool-down
- **Recovery:** No quality work for 5-7 days after. "Otherwise, we go to kill somebody."

**Canova's Marathon-Specific Track Workouts (Specific Phase):**

Example from Wilson Kipsang (before 2:03:23 WR):
- 2x3000m (8:42, 8:45), then 3x2000m (5:42-5:45), then 5x1000m (2:45-2:48), then 6x500m (1:20, last in 1:17)
- Recovery: 3 min between longer reps, 1:30 between 1Ks, 1:00 between 500s
- Total: 20km on the track

**Fartlek with Fast Recovery -- The Game-Changer:**

Canova's major innovation: During fartlek, the "recovery" jog is NOT a jog -- it's a RUN. Fast recoveries between surges improve cell membrane permeability, accelerating lactate clearance. This creates the ability to surge and recover within a marathon -- the defining skill of elite marathoners.

> "With the variation, something happens inside the body. The permeability of the cell membrane becomes better. We can make faster the possibility to eliminate lactate."

Example: 20x (1 min fast / 1 min "recovery" at good pace). Total: 1 hour, ~19.2km at 2300m altitude.

**Canova's Key Principles:**
1. **"The volume of intensity" is the key metric** -- not total volume, not peak intensity, but how much running you do at quality paces.
2. **Lower mileage, higher modulation.** Today's elites run 170-200km/week max (not 300+ like the 1980s). The key is bigger differences between hard and easy days.
3. **"Many small stairs, not big stairs."** Canova uses many adjacent speeds in training. He rejects the typical "easy / threshold / VO2max / sprint" framework as creating disconnected training stimuli. Instead, he prescribes many speeds that smoothly connect.
4. **"Add what you didn't do."** When taking on a new athlete, Canova doesn't change what works -- he adds what's missing.
5. **Top athletes must produce quality.** The specific phase is the most important. Being ready to "start training for the marathon" (end of fundamental phase) is different from being ready to "race the marathon" (end of specific phase).

**Sub-Elite Adaptations (40-70 miles/week):**

| Weekly Mileage | % at Marathon-Specific Speeds | Special Block Volume |
|----------------|------------------------------|---------------------|
| 40-50 mi/week | 50-60% | 19-22 miles total |
| 50-70 mi/week | 40-50% | 22-25 miles total |
| 70+ mi/week | 30-40% | 25-28 miles total |

**Relevance for AI Coach:** Canova's "extension of quality" philosophy should inform our marathon-specific training. The concept of "volume of intensity" is a better metric than weekly mileage alone. His speed-continuum approach (many small stairs) is superior to rigid zone-based training. The AI coach should track how much quality running a user does, not just total miles.

### 2.4 Brad Hudson

**Who:** Coach, author of "Run Faster from the 5K to the Marathon." Former competitive runner. Coached Dathan Ritzenhein and other elite Americans. Known for the "Adaptive Running" methodology.

**Core Philosophy: No Single Formula Works for Everyone**

Hudson's 12 adaptive running methods are built around one principle: observe the athlete's response and adjust constantly. He rejects rigid plans.

**Six Key Adaptive Techniques:**

1. **Targeted training mix:** Customize the balance of endurance, threshold, VO2max, and speed work to the individual's strengths and weaknesses.
2. **Progressive specificity:** Start with general fitness and progressively shift toward race-specific work.
3. **Controlled overreaching:** Periodically push volume or intensity beyond sustainable levels for a short block, then recover. This triggers supercompensation.
4. **Responsive scheduling:** Adjust the plan based on daily readiness. If an athlete feels flat, modify the workout rather than forcing it.
5. **Multi-pace long runs:** Long runs that incorporate tempo segments, marathon-pace blocks, or progression finishes.
6. **Racing as training:** Use races as fitness-building tools, not just tests.

**Hudson's Workouts (Canova-Influenced):**

Hudson draws heavily from Canova but makes the workouts more accessible for non-elite runners:
- **Progression long run:** Start 60-90 sec/mile slower than MP, finish at or near MP
- **Marathon-pace intervals:** 4-6 x 2 miles at MP with 800m jog recovery
- **Fartlek with purpose:** 10-12 x (2 min hard / 1 min moderate) -- NOT jogging the recovery

**Relevance for AI Coach:** Hudson's adaptive approach is essentially what our AI coach should be. His "responsive scheduling" principle is exactly what AI can do better than a static plan -- adjust in real-time based on performance, fatigue signals, and athlete feedback.

### 2.5 Pete Pfitzinger

**Who:** Exercise physiologist, two-time US Olympic marathoner (1984, 1988). Author of "Advanced Marathoning" and "Faster Road Racing." Known for bringing lab-tested physiology to practical training plans.

**Philosophy: The Lab-to-Road Approach**

Pfitzinger's plans are grounded in exercise physiology research. His hallmark is the **medium-long run** -- a moderately long run (typically 11-15 miles) done at a moderate effort mid-week, in addition to the traditional weekend long run. This significantly increases aerobic stimulus without the recovery cost of a true long run.

**Key Pfitzinger Principles:**

1. **Two "long" efforts per week:** The traditional long run (18-23 miles) PLUS a medium-long run (11-15 miles). This is the signature of Pfitzinger plans and a major differentiator.
2. **Lactate threshold running is king.** Pfitzinger considers tempo runs / threshold work the single most important workout for marathon performance.
3. **VO2max intervals for ceiling.** 5-6 x 1000m or 4-5 x 1200m at I pace, typically once per week in the build phase.
4. **Marathon-pace runs are the reality check.** 8-14 miles at MP, typically in the final 6-8 weeks.
5. **Progressive overload with recovery weeks.** Every 3-4 weeks, reduce mileage by ~20% before building again.
6. **General aerobic (GA) runs:** Moderate effort runs (not easy, not tempo) that build aerobic fitness. Pfitzinger is one of the few coaches who explicitly programs this "in-between" effort.

**Pfitzinger 18/70 Plan Structure (18 weeks, peaking at ~70 miles/week):**

The plan progresses through three rough phases:

**Weeks 1-6 (Endurance + LT Focus):**
- Peak: ~60-65 miles/week
- Long runs: 15-18 miles
- Medium-long runs: 11-13 miles
- 1x LT/tempo workout per week (4-7 miles at T pace)
- 1x VO2max or GA run
- Easy runs fill the rest

**Weeks 7-12 (Building + Race-Specific):**
- Peak: 67-70 miles/week
- Long runs: 18-20 miles (some with MP segments)
- Medium-long runs: 13-15 miles
- 1x LT workout, 1x VO2max session, 1x MP run
- Quality density increases

**Weeks 13-18 (Specific + Taper):**
- Week 13-15: Peak mileage with longest long runs (20-22 miles)
- Long runs now include 10-14 miles at MP
- Week 16-18: Progressive taper. Volume drops by 20%, 40%, 60%.
- Final week: ~30-35 miles with a few strides

**Typical Pfitzinger Week (Peak, ~70 miles):**

| Day | Workout | Distance |
|-----|---------|----------|
| Monday | Rest or cross-training | 0 |
| Tuesday | Medium-long run (GA effort) | 12 mi |
| Wednesday | VO2max intervals (5x1000m w/ 400m jog) | 10 mi |
| Thursday | Recovery + strides | 7 mi |
| Friday | LT run (8 mi w/ 5 mi at T pace) | 10 mi |
| Saturday | Recovery | 5 mi |
| Sunday | Long run | 20 mi |

**Relevance for AI Coach:** Pfitzinger's medium-long run concept is brilliant and underused. Our AI coach should incorporate it. His progressive taper protocol is well-researched. The GA-effort concept acknowledges that there's useful territory between "easy" and "tempo" -- our coach should prescribe this.

### 2.6 Keith and Kevin Hanson (Hansons Marathon Method)

**Who:** Brothers, coaches of the Hansons-Brooks Distance Project. Their "Hansons Marathon Method" book popularized the concept of cumulative fatigue training. Coached multiple US Olympic Trials qualifiers.

**Core Philosophy: Cumulative Fatigue**

The Hansons Method is built on one central idea: **You don't need to run 20+ miles in training because the purpose of the long run isn't to simulate the marathon -- it's to simulate the END of the marathon.** If you're running 50+ miles per week with quality sessions, your 16-mile long run starts on tired legs and the final miles simulate miles 20-26 of the race.

**Key Hansons Principles:**

1. **Long run capped at 16 miles.** This is the most controversial element. The logic: if you ran 10 miles earlier in the week, a 16-mile long run on fatigued legs is physiologically equivalent to miles 10-26 of a marathon.
2. **Six days per week of running.** High frequency, lower individual volume. No day off means more consistent training stimulus.
3. **Three key workouts per week:** Speed/strength, tempo, and the long run. These are non-negotiable. Everything else is easy running.
4. **Something of substance (SOS) days.** The three quality sessions. Each serves a specific purpose:
   - **Speed/Strength:** 12x400m or 6x800m at 5K-10K pace. Builds VO2max and running economy.
   - **Tempo:** 6-10 miles at marathon pace. THE most important workout. Teaches the body what race pace feels like.
   - **Long run:** 16 miles at easy-to-moderate effort. Builds endurance on fatigued legs.
5. **Easy days are truly easy.** Hansons are adamant: easy means easy. 1-2 min/mile slower than MP.

**Hansons Beginner Plan (18 weeks, peaks at ~50 miles/week):**

**Weeks 1-5 (Foundation):**
- Build to 35-40 miles/week
- 4-5 easy runs per week
- Long run builds from 10 to 14 miles
- Introduce strides 2x/week

**Weeks 6-10 (Strength):**
- 40-45 miles/week
- Speed work introduced: 8x600m, 6x800m at 5K-10K pace
- Tempo runs start: 5-6 miles at MP
- Long run: 14-16 miles

**Weeks 11-15 (Stamina):**
- 45-53 miles/week (peak)
- Tempo extends to 8-10 miles at MP
- Speed work continues
- Long run: 16 miles

**Weeks 16-18 (Taper):**
- Volume drops to 35-40 miles
- Quality maintained but volume of quality sessions decreases
- Final long run: 10 miles, two weeks out

**Relevance for AI Coach:** The cumulative fatigue concept is sound and should inform our coach's logic. Rather than obsessing over long-run distance, the AI should consider total weekly load and the fatigue state entering key workouts. The 6-day-per-week high-frequency approach works well for runners with limited time per session. The tempo-at-marathon-pace emphasis aligns with both Canova and Pfitzinger.

### 2.7 Gjert Ingebrigtsen

**Who:** Norwegian coach, father of Henrik, Filip, and Jakob Ingebrigtsen. Self-taught coach who developed a system that produced one of the greatest middle-distance runners in history (Jakob -- Olympic 1500m gold, multiple world records).

**The Norwegian Method (as practiced by the Ingebrigtsen family):**

The "Norwegian Method" is really a set of principles, not a single protocol:

1. **Double threshold training:** 2x/week, the athlete performs two threshold sessions in one day (morning and evening). This accumulates more threshold volume than traditional single sessions.
2. **Lactate-controlled intensity:** Workouts are controlled by blood lactate, typically targeting 2.0-3.5 mmol/L (below the commonly cited 4.0 threshold). The goal: maximum aerobic stimulus with minimum muscular stress.
3. **Threshold over speed.** The Ingebrigtsen system prioritizes raising the anaerobic threshold above all else. Speed work is secondary and added closer to competition.
4. **High total volume:** Jakob runs ~180km/week. The easy runs are genuinely easy.
5. **The "X element":** Once per week, a session of higher intensity (hill work, race-pace intervals, or races). This provides the speed stimulus.

**Typical Ingebrigtsen Double-Threshold Week:**

| Day | AM Session | PM Session |
|-----|-----------|------------|
| Monday | Easy distance (15km) | Easy distance (12km) |
| Tuesday | Threshold session #1 (e.g., 6x6min @ LT, 1min rest) | Threshold session #2 (e.g., 20x45sec @ LT, 15sec rest) + Strength |
| Wednesday | Easy distance (16km) | Easy distance (10km) |
| Thursday | Threshold session #3 | Threshold session #4 |
| Friday | Easy distance (15km) | Easy distance (9km) + Strength |
| Saturday | "X element" (hills, race-pace work) | Easy distance (10km) |
| Sunday | Long easy run (21km) | -- |

**Typical Threshold Workouts for Jakob Ingebrigtsen:**
- 10 x 1000m at LT with 1:00 rest
- 20 x 400m at LT with 0:30 rest
- 6 x 6 minutes at LT with 1:00 rest
- 10 x 3 minutes at LT with 45 seconds rest

**The Key Insight (from Marius Bakken, originator of the approach):**

> "Lowering the lactate level from the standard 4.0 mmol/L down to 2.3-3.0 on sessions gave not only far better results, you could also do huge amounts of threshold training -- substantially more versus a level close to 4.0, without wearing down."

The short rest between morning and evening sessions allows muscle tone to reset. The cardiovascular and metabolic systems don't need long recovery -- it's the muscles that limit you. By splitting into two sessions, you get more total threshold volume with less muscular damage.

**Relevance for AI Coach:** For most recreational athletes, double-threshold days are impractical and risky. But the principle -- accumulate more threshold work at a controlled intensity -- is universally applicable. Our AI coach should prescribe more frequent, moderate-intensity threshold sessions rather than fewer, harder ones. Two threshold workouts per week is achievable for most runners, just not on the same day.

### 2.8 Alberto Salazar and the Nike Oregon Project

**Who:** Alberto Salazar (b. 1958), former American marathon record holder, head coach of the Nike Oregon Project (NOP) from 2001-2019. The NOP trained Mo Farah, Galen Rupp, and other world-class athletes. Salazar received a 4-year ban from USADA in 2019 for anti-doping violations (trafficking testosterone, misusing IV infusions, and tampering with doping controls).

**Training Methods (separated from the doping controversy):**

The NOP's training approach -- independent of pharmacological manipulation -- was studied and influenced many programs:

1. **Technology integration:** The NOP pioneered the use of altitude tents, underwater treadmills, anti-gravity treadmills (AlterG), and real-time biomechanical analysis. Every possible legal edge was pursued.
2. **Altitude training:** Year-round altitude exposure through live-high-train-low protocols and altitude tents.
3. **Strength and power emphasis:** More strength training than traditional distance programs. Included weight room work, plyometrics, and sprint training.
4. **High specificity:** Race-pace workouts were a major emphasis, especially in the final 6-8 weeks before competition.
5. **Aggressive recovery:** Ice baths, NormaTec compression, massage, and careful periodization of recovery modalities.

**The Cautionary Lesson:**

The NOP story is a reminder that performance enhancement exists on a spectrum. The legitimate training innovations (technology, strength work, altitude) are valuable. The line-crossing (doping) is not. For our AI coach, the lesson is: pursue every legal edge with rigor, and never promise shortcuts that don't exist.

**Relevance for AI Coach:** The technology integration lessons are directly applicable. Our coach should leverage wearable data aggressively. The strength + altitude emphasis is validated by subsequent research. The cautionary element: never promise "hacks" or unsupported supplements.

## 3. Elite Marathoners -- What They Actually Do

### 3.1 Eliud Kipchoge

**Coach:** Patrick Sang (former Olympic steeplechase silver medalist)

**The System:**
- **Location:** Kaptagat, Kenya (7,870 feet elevation)
- **Training blocks:** 16 weeks (typically), preceded by 3-4 weeks completely off, then 3-4 weeks of preparatory work
- **Weekly mileage:** 124-136 miles per week (200-220km)
- **Training sessions:** 13 per week (2x daily every day except Sunday)
- **Effort distribution:** "I try not to run 100 percent. I perform 80 percent on Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday and then at 50 percent Monday, Wednesday, Friday, Sunday."

**Kipchoge's Standard Training Week:**

| Day | AM Session | PM Session |
|-----|-----------|------------|
| Monday | 12 miles easy | 6 miles easy |
| Tuesday | Track workout: 9-10 miles of speed (e.g., 8x1600m in 4:40 w/ 2min rest; or 8x400m in 63-64s w/ 30-50s rest) | 6 miles easy |
| Wednesday | 12 miles easy | 6 miles easy + 60min strength/mobility |
| Thursday | 19 or 25-mile run (alternating weeks) | -- |
| Friday | 12 miles easy | 6 miles easy + 60min strength/mobility |
| Saturday | Fartlek: 13x3min fast / 1min jog | 6 miles easy |
| Sunday | 2 hours easy | -- |

**Key Observations:**

1. **Simplicity is the defining feature.** No periodized phases with different names. Slight variations of the same workouts for 16 weeks.
2. **Three hard days, four easy days.** Every week. No exceptions.
3. **Recovery runs start at 8:30-8:45/mile and build to 6:30-7:00/mile.** Even at 4:38 marathon pace, easy days are genuinely slow.
4. **Strength work is minimal but consistent.** 2x60-minute sessions/week focused on glutes, hamstrings, core. Resistance bands and bodyweight only. No heavy lifting.
5. **Recovery is treated as a discipline.** 5:45 AM wake, 9 PM bedtime. 1-hour daily nap. Three liters of water. No supplements. Massage 2x/week.
6. **Progress by feel, not GPS.** Patrick Sang: "I like my athletes to monitor effort -- not with GPS watches or heart-rate monitors but by feel." Pace should naturally increase each week as fitness builds.
7. **Training logs are handwritten.** Kipchoge logs every detail: time, kilometers, massage, exercises, shoes, shoe feel. 18 notebooks spanning his career.

**Patrick Sang's Coaching Philosophy:**

> "What's the biggest mistake marathoners make? Not listening to their bodies very well. And not understanding what they are supposed to do."

> "If you hammer the first half of a long run but can't maintain it to the end... you're on my list."

### 3.2 Kelvin Kiptum (1999-2024, RIP)

**Coach:** Gervais Hakizimana (Rwandan former runner)

**The Record:** 2:00:35 at Chicago Marathon 2023, shattering Kipchoge's record by 34 seconds. Only three career marathons before his death in a car accident in February 2024.

**Training Philosophy -- Extreme Volume:**

> "All he does is run, eat, sleep." -- Gervais Hakizimana

- **Weekly mileage:** 250-300+ km per week (155-186 miles). Three weeks above 300km in London prep.
- **Comparison:** Kipchoge runs 180-220km. Kiptum exceeded this by 40-80%.
- **No days off.** "There is no weekly rest. We rest when he gets tired. If for a month he doesn't show signs of fatigue or pain, we continue."

**Kiptum's Weekly Structure:**

| Day | Training |
|-----|---------|
| Mon/Wed/Fri | 40km total: 25-28km AM + 12km PM |
| Tuesday | Track fartlek sessions |
| Thursday | 40km at marathon pace (near a full marathon distance!) |
| Saturday | Track or road sessions |
| Sunday | 40km at marathon pace |

**The Risk:**

Even Hakizimana feared this approach:
> "At this rate he risks breaking. I suggested he lower the pace but he doesn't want to. I told him that in five years he would be done."

**What Made Kiptum Different:**
1. **No track background.** Went straight to marathons, unlike virtually every other elite marathoner.
2. **Mostly self-trained** until formalizing with Hakizimana in early 2023.
3. **Pure volume approach** -- less about workout sophistication, more about relentless mileage.
4. **Youth:** Only 23 when he set the WR, suggesting enormous natural talent and resilience.

**Relevance for AI Coach:** Kiptum is an outlier, not a model. His extreme volume is not replicable for 99.99% of runners and even his coach warned of breakdown risk. The lesson for our coach: some athletes thrive on volume, and the system should be flexible enough to accommodate high-volume approaches. But the default should be Kipchoge's more sustainable model.

### 3.5 Des Linden

**Who:** 2018 Boston Marathon champion (first American woman in 33 years, in brutal Nor'easter conditions). Two-time Olympian.

**Training Philosophy: Show Up Every Day**

> "There's only one rule for success: keep showing up."

**The Linden Approach:**
- Trained with the Hansons-Brooks Distance Project for 13 years under Keith and Kevin Hanson, then moved to coach Walt Drenth (her college coach) for later career
- **Consistency is everything.** Linden is not the most talented runner in any given field, but she is among the most consistent. She grinds through winters in Michigan and shows up for every session.
- **Key workout example (pre-marathon):** 5 miles at marathon pace, 4min rest, 1 mile hard (4:49), 4min rest, 5 miles at marathon pace.

## 4. Training Plan Structures

### 4.1 Jack Daniels 5K Training Plan (Sample, 12-18 weeks)

**Phase I (Weeks 1-4): Foundation**
- 5-6 days/week running
- All easy pace (E)
- Strides 4-6x100m after 2-3 easy runs per week
- Build weekly mileage gradually

**Phase II (Weeks 5-8): Early Quality**
- 2 quality sessions per week
- Session 1: R-pace work (200m-400m repeats at R pace, full recovery)
- Session 2: I-pace intervals (3-5 x 1000m at I pace with equal-time jog rest)
- Long run 25-30% of weekly mileage
- Example: 5x1000m @ 6:50 pace (VDOT 45) w/ 3-4 min jog

**Phase III (Weeks 9-14): Threshold Development**
- 2-3 quality sessions per week
- Session 1: T-pace work (tempo runs 20-30min, or cruise intervals 4-5x6min w/ 1min rest)
- Session 2: I-pace intervals (now longer: 5-6 x 1000m)
- Session 3 (optional): R-pace sprints for economy
- Example: 4x2000m @ 7:25 pace (VDOT 45) w/ 2min rest

**Phase IV (Weeks 15-18): Race Specific**
- 2 quality sessions per week
- Session 1: Race-specific combo (e.g., 2x1600 @ I pace + 4x400 @ R pace)
- Session 2: T-pace or race simulation
- Volume decreases slightly
- Tune-up race at week 16

**Final Week Taper:**
- 60% of normal volume
- One short quality session (4x400 @ I pace on Tuesday)
- Easy runs only Wed-Fri
- Race Saturday or Sunday

### 4.2 Pfitzinger 18/70 Marathon Plan

**See section 2.5 for detailed breakdown.**

Summary of weekly mileage progression:
- Weeks 1-4: 53-62 miles
- Weeks 5-8: 58-67 miles
- Weeks 9-12: 61-70 miles (peak)
- Weeks 13-15: 63-70 miles (peak long runs)
- Weeks 16-18: 55 > 40 > 30 miles (taper)

Key workout types across the plan:
- **Endurance runs:** 15-22 miles at easy-to-moderate pace
- **Medium-long runs:** 11-15 miles at GA effort (mid-week)
- **LT runs:** 7-11 miles total, with 4-7 miles at threshold
- **VO2max intervals:** 5-6 x 600-1200m at I pace
- **Marathon pace runs:** 8-15 miles including 5-14 miles at MP
- **Recovery runs:** 4-7 miles easy

### 4.3 Hansons Beginner Marathon Plan

**See section 2.6 for detailed breakdown.**

Key differences from Pfitzinger:
- Long run capped at 16 miles
- 6 days/week running (vs Pfitzinger's 5-6)
- No medium-long run concept -- uses cumulative fatigue instead
- Tempo runs are at marathon pace (not threshold)

### 4.4 Lydiard 6-Month Periodization

**See section 2.2 for detailed breakdown.**

The critical sequence:
1. **14-16 weeks of aerobic base** (100+ miles/week for serious runners)
2. **4-6 weeks of hill resistance** (hill circuits 3x/week)
3. **4-6 weeks of anaerobic/track** (intervals at race pace and faster)
4. **2-4 weeks of coordination** (race simulations, tune-ups)
5. **10-14 days taper**

The key Lydiard insight: steps 2-5 can be compressed or extended, but step 1 cannot be shortened. Aerobic base is the non-negotiable foundation.

### 4.5 Norwegian Double-Threshold Week

**See section 2.7 for detailed breakdown.**

For a non-elite runner adapting the principles (training 6x/week):

| Day | Session |
|-----|---------|
| Monday | Easy run (45-60 min) |
| Tuesday | Threshold intervals: 6x1000m @ LT with 1:00 jog rest |
| Wednesday | Easy run (45-60 min) |
| Thursday | Threshold intervals: 5x6 min @ LT with 1:30 jog rest |
| Friday | Easy run (30-40 min) + strength |
| Saturday | "X element": Hill repeats or race-pace work |
| Sunday | Long easy run (90-120 min) |

The adaptation: instead of double-threshold within one day, spread two threshold sessions across different days. Same weekly threshold volume, lower per-session stress.

## 5. What the Best Actually Do -- Universal Patterns

### 5.1 Typical Weekly Structure for a Sub-Elite Marathoner (60-80 miles/week)

| Day | Session | Purpose |
|-----|---------|---------|
| Monday | Rest or easy 4-6 miles | Recovery |
| Tuesday | Quality #1: Intervals or tempo (10-12 miles total) | VO2max or LT development |
| Wednesday | Medium-long run (12-14 miles, moderate effort) | Aerobic stimulus |
| Thursday | Easy 6-8 miles + strides | Recovery + neuromuscular |
| Friday | Quality #2: Tempo or MP work (10-12 miles total) | Race-specific fitness |
| Saturday | Easy 5-7 miles | Pre-long-run recovery |
| Sunday | Long run (18-22 miles) | Endurance, fat oxidation, mental toughness |

### 5.2 Typical Weekly Structure for an Elite 5K/10K Runner (80-100km/week)

| Day | Session | Purpose |
|-----|---------|---------|
| Monday | Easy run AM + easy run PM | Recovery, volume |
| Tuesday | VO2max intervals: 6-8x1000m @ I pace | Ceiling development |
| Wednesday | Easy run + strides or drills | Recovery |
| Thursday | Threshold work: 6x6min @ LT or 25min tempo | Lactate clearance |
| Friday | Easy run + strength training | Recovery + injury prevention |
| Saturday | Race-pace work or race | Speed, competition |
| Sunday | Long run (75-90 min) | Aerobic base maintenance |

### 5.3 How Elites Periodize Across a Season

Most elite runners follow a macro-periodization model with 2-3 racing peaks per year:

**Off-Season (4-6 weeks post-goal race):**
- Complete rest (1-2 weeks)
- Easy running only, gradually increasing
- Strength training emphasis
- Cross-training allowed

**Base Phase (8-14 weeks):**
- Volume builds to peak levels
- All running is easy to moderate
- Some strides and light speedwork
- Long runs extend in duration

**Build Phase (6-8 weeks):**
- Quality sessions introduced (2-3 per week)
- Volume maintained or slightly reduced
- Threshold, VO2max, and race-pace workouts
- Tune-up races

**Peak/Specific Phase (4-6 weeks):**
- Race-specific workouts dominate quality days
- Volume decreases 10-20%
- Intensity of quality sessions highest of the cycle
- Final tune-up race 3-4 weeks out

**Taper (2-3 weeks):**
- Volume drops 40-60% over 2-3 weeks
- Intensity maintained in short, sharp sessions
- Extra sleep, nutrition focus
- Race week: minimal running, a few strides

### 5.4 Recovery Practices of Elite Runners

**Sleep:**
- **Kipchoge:** 5:45 AM - 9:00 PM (9.25 hours) + 1 hour daily nap = 10+ hours total
- **Most elites:** 8-10 hours of night sleep + 30-90 minute afternoon nap
- **Research consensus:** Sleep is the #1 recovery modality. Everything else is secondary.

**Nutrition:**
- Most elites eat simple, whole foods. The Kenyan diet: ugali (maize flour porridge), vegetables, fruit, tea, some meat
- Protein timing post-workout is emphasized (30-40g within 2 hours)
- Carbohydrate availability is managed -- higher on hard days, lower on easy days (natural periodization)
- Most elite runners do NOT take many supplements. Kipchoge takes none.
- Hydration: 2-3+ liters of water daily

**Active Recovery:**
- Easy jogging (the PM session for double-day runners IS active recovery)
- Walking
- Pool running/aqua jogging (non-impact)

**Massage and Manual Therapy:**
- Most elites get massage 2-3x/week
- Kipchoge's physio Peter Nduhiu has worked with him since 2003
- Focus on maintaining tissue quality, not deep-tissue punishment

**Cold and Heat Therapy:**
- Ice baths post-long runs or hard sessions: 10-15 minutes at 50-59F (10-15C)
- Contrast therapy (alternating hot/cold) gaining popularity
- **Important nuance:** Cold exposure immediately after strength training may blunt adaptation. Best reserved for after hard runs, not after gym sessions.

**Technology/Modalities:**
- Compression boots (NormaTec)
- Foam rolling and lacrosse ball work
- Elevation of legs post-run

### 5.5 The Role of Strength Training in Elite Running

**The Evidence:**
- A comprehensive meta-analysis found strength training reduces sports injuries by nearly two-thirds and cuts overuse injury incidence
- Research shows strength training improves running economy (less oxygen needed at the same pace) by 2-8%
- No evidence that distance runners who don't strength train are MORE injured, but strong evidence that those who DO are less injured

**What Elites Actually Do:**

**Kipchoge's approach (minimal):**
- 2x60 min/week
- Bodyweight and resistance bands only
- Glute abduction, bridges, planks, single-leg deadlifts, proprioception exercises
- "The exercises are not something where you suffer. The idea is to create basic balance in the body."

**Jakob Ingebrigtsen (more substantial):**
- 2x/week strength sessions
- Includes weights (squats, deadlifts)
- Plyometrics and hill bounding
- Higher strength emphasis than most middle-distance runners

**Des Linden (moderate):**
- 2x/week after hard sessions
- Bodyweight: push-ups, lunges, planks, sit-ups, squats
- "Not all that complex"

**Canova's circuit approach (integrated):**
- Strength training embedded INTO running workouts
- 400m at MP, immediately into 30sec of bounding/skipping/squat jumps, repeat
- "We don't need high strength for a marathon runner. We need strength endurance."

**Consensus for recreational runners:**
- 2x/week, 20-40 minutes per session
- Focus: single-leg exercises (lunges, step-ups, single-leg deadlifts), hip stability, core
- Heavy compounds (squats, deadlifts) are beneficial but not required
- Plyometrics (box jumps, bounding) improve running economy but require a strength base first
- Do strength work AFTER running or on separate days, not before hard running sessions

### 5.6 Altitude Training and Why East Africans Dominate

**Why Kenyans and Ethiopians Win:**

Research has identified four primary factors:

1. **Altitude exposure from birth.** Kenya's Rift Valley (2000-2500m) and Ethiopia's highlands provide natural altitude training. Living at altitude increases red blood cell production, hemoglobin concentration, and oxygen-carrying capacity.

2. **Biomechanical advantages.** Kalenjin runners (the tribe producing most Kenyan champions) tend to have long, thin legs with narrow calves and ankles. This reduces the energy cost of swinging the leg forward -- better running economy.

3. **Training culture and motivation.** Running is a path out of poverty. Young Kenyans run to school (often miles), observe elite runners in their communities, and enter a deeply established training pipeline. Group training culture creates mutual accountability.

4. **Simple, whole-food nutrition.** High-carb, moderate-protein diet. Ugali, vegetables, tea. Low in processed foods. No supplement dependence.

**Altitude Training for Non-East-Africans:**

The "live high, train low" model is considered optimal:
- **Live at 7,000-8,500 feet** (2100-2600m) for minimum 4 weeks
- **Train at lower altitude** when possible for quality sessions (to maintain speed/intensity)
- Benefits: increased EPO production, higher hemoglobin, improved oxygen delivery
- Drawbacks: sleep disruption at altitude, reduced training quality if you can't descend for workouts
- **Altitude tents:** Simulate living at altitude while sleeping at home. Used widely in elite programs (NOP, British Athletics, etc.)

## 6. Key Takeaways for Our AI Coach

### 6.1 Universal Truths ALL Great Coaches Agree On

These are non-negotiable principles that every major coaching tradition supports:

1. **Most running should be easy.** Whether you call it 80/20 (Daniels, Fitzgerald), Zone 2 (Galpin), or "50% effort" (Kipchoge), the consensus is clear: 75-85% of training volume should be at conversational, genuinely easy effort. The #1 mistake runners make is running easy days too hard.

2. **Consistency beats intensity.** Lydiard, Linden, Flanagan, Kipchoge, and every other enduring champion trained consistently over years. Missing one workout doesn't matter. Missing weeks of training because of injury from overreaching does.

3. **Specificity matters.** You get good at what you practice. Marathon training requires marathon-pace work. 5K training requires 5K-pace work. General fitness is a foundation, not a destination.

4. **Progressive overload is the mechanism of improvement.** Gradually increase the training stress (volume, intensity, or both) over time. The body adapts to the stimulus it's given.

5. **Recovery is training.** Sleep, nutrition, and easy days are not "time off" -- they're when adaptation happens. Kipchoge is very good at doing nothing. Galpin: sleep is the most powerful recovery tool.

6. **Strength training belongs in every program.** Not for bulk -- for injury prevention and running economy. Even Kipchoge does it.

7. **Listen to the body.** Patrick Sang, Des Linden, Brad Hudson, Molly Seidel's coach Jon Green -- all emphasize reading daily readiness and adjusting. Rigid adherence to a plan that ignores fatigue signals leads to injury.

8. **The long run is non-negotiable.** Whether it's 16 miles (Hansons) or 25 miles (Kipchoge), or 40km at pace (Kiptum), every tradition includes a regularly occurring extended-duration effort.

9. **Threshold work is the most important quality session.** Daniels, Pfitzinger, the Norwegians, Canova, Hudson -- all place lactate threshold development at or near the top of the quality hierarchy for distances from 5K to marathon.

10. **Taper works.** Every tradition reduces volume before goal races. Typically 2-3 weeks with 40-60% volume reduction while maintaining some intensity.

### 6.2 Genuine Disagreements Among Great Coaches

1. **Long run distance.**
   - Hansons: 16 miles max (cumulative fatigue theory)
   - Pfitzinger: 20-22 miles (traditional approach)
   - Kipchoge/Canova: 25+ miles / 40km at pace (elite-specific)
   - **Our AI coach should:** Scale long run distance to the individual's weekly mileage and experience. 25-30% of weekly mileage is a good guideline.

2. **Speed of easy runs.**
   - Canova/Kipchoge tradition: Start very slow (8:30+ pace for a 4:38 marathoner) and build
   - Some traditions allow "general aerobic" effort (Pfitzinger's GA pace) on easy days
   - **Our AI coach should:** Default to genuinely easy. If the athlete is running easy days too fast (detected via HR or pace data), alert them.

3. **The value of "junk miles" / Zone 3.**
   - Galpin/80-20 school: Zone 3 is "no man's land" -- avoid it
   - Canova: No such thing as wasted speed. "Many small stairs" means some running between easy and threshold is valuable.
   - **Our AI coach should:** Generally avoid prescribing Zone 3 work, but recognize that some moderate-effort running (GA runs) has a place, especially for experienced runners.

4. **Periodization model.**
   - Lydiard: Strictly sequential (base > hills > track > racing)
   - Daniels: Phase-based with overlap
   - Canova: Overlapping phases with early intensity
   - Norwegian: Year-round threshold emphasis
   - **Our AI coach should:** Use a modified Daniels phase model for most users, with Canova-inspired specificity for experienced marathoners.

5. **Volume vs. intensity.**
   - Kiptum: Extreme volume (300km/week), less workout sophistication
   - Norwegian model: Moderate volume (180km), extreme threshold accumulation
   - **Our AI coach should:** Calibrate to the individual. More experienced runners with more time can benefit from higher volume. Time-limited runners should maximize quality within their available volume.

6. **The role of VO2max work.**
   - Daniels: Critical for all distances, prescribed year-round
   - Norwegian model: Secondary to threshold; added closer to competition
   - Canova: Embedded in race-specific work rather than isolated intervals
   - **Our AI coach should:** Include VO2max intervals, especially for 5K-10K training. For marathon training, VO2max work is more important in early phases, giving way to threshold and MP work in later phases.

### 6.3 What an AI Coach Should Do Differently Than a Static Plan

1. **Adjust in real-time based on performance data.** When the athlete's HR is elevated on an easy run, the AI should ask "are you feeling recovered?" and potentially modify the next workout. This is Brad Hudson's "adaptive running" automated.

2. **Recalculate paces from recent performances.** The VDOT system updates every 4-6 weeks from race results. Our AI should also update from workout data -- if tempo runs are getting easier at a given pace, it's time to adjust upward.

3. **Detect the overtraining spiral before it becomes injury.** Track trends: rising resting HR, declining pace at same HR, poor sleep, skipped workouts, negative sentiment in check-ins. Intervene with reduced volume before the athlete breaks down.

4. **Personalize the plan structure to the individual's life.** A parent with a toddler doesn't need Kipchoge's 13-session week. They need a plan that fits 4-5 sessions around a chaotic schedule, maximizing quality in limited time. Static plans can't do this.

5. **Communicate the WHY behind every workout.** Every run in the plan should have a clear purpose. "Today's easy run builds aerobic base and promotes recovery from yesterday's intervals" is more motivating than "Easy: 6 miles." Galpin, Daniels, and Canova all emphasize that understanding purpose improves execution.

6. **Scale intensity prescriptions individually.** Don't give a beginning runner threshold intervals on Day 1. Build up gradually. But also don't baby an experienced runner who can handle Norwegian-style threshold volume. Match the prescription to the athlete's history.

7. **Incorporate periodization intelligence.** Know where the athlete is in their training cycle and adjust the training mix accordingly. Early phases: more volume, less intensity. Late phases: more specificity, less general work. Taper: less volume, maintained intensity.

8. **Make the easy days genuinely easy.** This is the hardest habit to enforce and the most impactful. The AI should actively monitor pace/HR on easy days and remind the athlete to slow down. Every coaching tradition agrees this is the #1 mistake.

9. **Include recovery modalities in the prescription.** Don't just prescribe running. Prescribe sleep targets, hydration reminders, strength sessions, and rest days. Recovery IS part of the training plan.

10. **Learn the athlete's response patterns over time.** Track which workouts the athlete responds well to, how many hard days per week they tolerate, how quickly they recover from long runs. Use this data to build a progressively more personalized experience.
